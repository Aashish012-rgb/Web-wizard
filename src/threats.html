Common Cyber Threats

Know the threats to protect yourself and your loved ones

Understanding Cyber Threats

Cyber threats are constantly evolving. Criminals use sophisticated techniques to trick you into revealing information or compromising your devices. The good news? Most threats can be prevented with knowledge and caution.

Key Understanding: Recognizing and understanding these threats is your first line of defense. An informed user is a protected user.

1. Phishing Attacks

ATTACK LEVEL: CRITICAL

What is Phishing?

Phishing is when attackers use deceptive emails, messages, or websites to trick you into revealing personal information, passwords, or clicking malicious links.

How Attackers Do It:

  • Create fake emails that look like they're from your bank, PayPal, or favorite service
  • Include official logos and branding to appear legitimate
  • Create urgency ("Your account will be closed in 24 hours!")
  • Include links that direct you to fake websites
  • Ask you to "verify" or "confirm" your information

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Never click links in unsolicited emails
  • Type the website URL directly into your browser
  • Check the sender's email address carefully
  • Look for grammar and spelling errors
  • Legitimate organizations never ask for passwords via email
  • Hover over links to see their real destination
  • Report phishing emails to your email provider

2. Malware

ATTACK LEVEL: CRITICAL

What is Malware?

Malware (malicious software) includes viruses, trojans, ransomware, and spyware that can damage your device or steal your information.

Viruses

Replicate themselves and damage files or systems. Often spread through email attachments.

Trojans

Disguise as legitimate software but contain hidden malicious code or backdoors.

Ransomware

Encrypts your files and demands payment to unlock them. Very dangerous.

Spyware

Secretly monitors your activities, stealing passwords and personal information.

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Install and maintain updated antivirus/anti-malware software
  • Keep your operating system and software updated
  • Don't open suspicious attachments or files
  • Download software only from official websites
  • Be cautious with torrents and file-sharing sites
  • Scan USB drives before using them
  • Regular backups can help recover from ransomware

3. Password Attacks

ATTACK LEVEL: HIGH

Types of Password Attacks

Brute Force Attacks:

Hackers use software to try thousands of password combinations until they find the right one.

Dictionary Attacks:

Attackers use common words and password patterns to guess your password.

Credential Stuffing:

Using leaked usernames and passwords from other websites to access your accounts.

Keylogging:

Malware records your keyboard input, capturing passwords as you type.

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Use strong, unique passwords (12+ characters, mixed case, symbols, numbers)
  • Don't reuse passwords across different sites
  • Use a password manager securely
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA)
  • Never share your password
  • Change passwords immediately if you suspect compromise

4. Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) Attacks

ATTACK LEVEL: HIGH

What is MITM?

An attacker intercepts communication between you and a website or service to steal data or impersonate one of the parties.

Common Scenarios:

  • Public WiFi network without encryption
  • Unsecured website (not HTTPS)
  • Compromised network router

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Only use HTTPS websites (look for padlock icon)
  • Avoid public WiFi for sensitive transactions
  • Use a VPN on public networks
  • Verify SSL certificates on websites
  • Don't access banking on public WiFi

5. Social Engineering

ATTACK LEVEL: HIGH

What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering exploits human psychology to trick you into revealing information or taking actions that compromise security.

Common Methods:

  • Pretexting: Attacker creates a false scenario to gain your trust
  • Baiting: Offering something attractive (USB stick, free software)
  • Quid Pro Quo: Promising something in exchange for information
  • Tailgating: Following someone into restricted areas
  • Reverse Social Engineering: Setting up a fake support scenario

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Be skeptical of unsolicited contact
  • Verify identities before sharing information
  • Don't assume emails are legitimate based on appearance
  • Be cautious about overly friendly online interactions
  • Report suspicious behavior
  • Trust your instincts

6. DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) Attacks

ATTACK LEVEL: MEDIUM (For Individuals)

What is DDoS?

A DDoS attack floods a website or service with so much traffic that legitimate users cannot access it.

While DDoS attacks primarily target websites and services rather than individual users, they can affect you if you rely on those services.

Protection (As a Service User):

  • Use services with DDoS protection
  • Have backup connection methods
  • Monitor service status
  • Contact service providers if you suspect attacks

7. Ransomware

ATTACK LEVEL: CRITICAL

What is Ransomware?

Ransomware encrypts your files and demands payment to decrypt them. It's one of the most dangerous threats today.

WARNING: NEVER pay ransoms. There's no guarantee your files will be recovered, and you'll fund future attacks.

How It Spreads:

  • Email attachments
  • Compromised websites
  • Malware downloads
  • Unpatched software vulnerabilities

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Keep regular, offline backups of important files
  • Update your operating system and software regularly
  • Don't click suspicious email links or attachments
  • Use updated antivirus software
  • Use strong passwords and 2FA
  • Disconnect from the internet if infected (to prevent spread)
  • Report infections to authorities

8. Identity Theft

ATTACK LEVEL: CRITICAL

What is Identity Theft?

Identity theft occurs when someone uses your personal information to commit fraud or other crimes.

How Thieves Operate:

  • Steal information from data breaches
  • Use phishing to collect information
  • Hack email or social media accounts
  • Purchase stolen data on the dark web

What They Can Do:

  • Open bank accounts or credit cards in your name
  • Take out loans
  • Apply for government benefits
  • Commit tax fraud

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Monitor your credit reports regularly
  • Place fraud alerts with credit bureaus
  • Keep personal information private
  • Shred sensitive documents
  • Use strong passwords and 2FA
  • Check financial statements regularly
  • Report suspicious activity immediately

9. Cyberbullying and Harassment

ATTACK LEVEL: HIGH

What is Cyberbullying?

Cyberbullying involves using digital platforms to harass, threaten, embarrass, or humiliate someone.

Forms of Cyberbullying:

  • Offensive messages or comments
  • Sharing private or embarrassing information
  • Impersonation
  • Spreading rumors or false information
  • Exclusion from online groups
  • Doxxing (sharing personal information)

How to Protect Yourself:

  • Don't engage with bullies; don't respond
  • Save evidence of harassment
  • Block and report harassers
  • Don't share sensitive information online
  • Privacy settings should be strict
  • Tell a trusted adult if you're being bullied
  • Report to the platform and authorities

Staying Protected: Quick Reference

Essential Protection Practices

Threat Type Best Defense
Phishing Think before clicking; verify senders
Malware Updated antivirus; don't open suspicious files
Password Attacks Strong unique passwords; 2FA
MITM Attacks HTTPS only; avoid public WiFi
Social Engineering Skepticism; verify identities
Ransomware Regular backups; keep software updated
Identity Theft Monitor credit; protect information
Cyberbullying Block; report; don't engage

If You've Already Been Compromised

  1. Disconnect: If possible, disconnect from the internet immediately
  2. Report: Report to relevant authorities and the platform where it occurred
  3. Change Passwords: Change passwords from a safe device
  4. Monitor: Watch for unauthorized activity on your accounts
  5. Seek Help: Contact cybercrime helplines and banking institutions if needed
  6. Documentation: Keep records of everything for law enforcement
Remember: Stay informed, stay vigilant, and don't hesitate to ask for help. Cybersecurity is a shared responsibility.
